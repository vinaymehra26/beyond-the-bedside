How To Get Prepared For NORCET

A complete guide to NORCET preparation — syllabus breakdown, exam pattern, and online courses.
May 31, 2026 · 4 min · Vinay Mehra

Understanding The Exam First#

NORCET is conducted by AIIMS New Delhi in a center-based online CBT format. It happens in two stages.

Stage I (Prelims) is the qualifying exam. Candidates who qualified this are later shortlisted for the stage II exam.

Stage II (Mains) is the merit or rank deciding exam. All candidates merit is decided by this exam which is used for allotting the vacancies of staff nurse that are made available.

Category Stage I (Prelims) Stage II (Mains)
Questions 100 MCQs 160 MCQs
Sections Nursing + GK & Aptitude 4 sections, 40 questions each
Time 90 minutes 180 minutes
Purpose Qualifying only Determines final merit & rank
Negative Marking -1/3 per wrong answer -1/3 per wrong answer

Qualifying cutoffs:

Category Cutoff
UR / EWS 50%
OBC 45%
SC / ST 40%

What Comes in the Exam?#

The exam syllabus mainly include all the nursing subjects that are taught in the Nursing undergraduate academic courses. Here is the all exam portion.

Core Nursing Subjects:

  1. Anatomy & Physiology
  2. Biochemistry & Nutrition
  3. Microbiology
  4. Fundamentals of Nursing
  5. Medical-Surgical Nursing (Adult Health Nursing)
  6. Community Health Nursing
  7. Mental Health Nursing
  8. Child Health Nursing
  9. Obstetric & Gynecological Nursing (Midwifery)
  10. Pharmacology
  11. Nursing Research & Statistics

General Awareness & Aptitude (Stage I only):

  1. Current Affairs
  2. General English
  3. Reasoning & Aptitude

Subjects that Carry Heavy Weightage#

  • Medical-Surgical Nursing (Adult Health Nursing)
  • Community Health Nursing
  • OBG (Midwifery)

These subjects appear heavily in the exam so giving them more preparation is beneficial for exam. While General Awareness & Aptitude will only come in Stage I and sometimes they can be qualification deciding so don’t ignore them completely in the preparation also.

Handling the Aptitude & GK Section#

Most nursing students make the same mistake with this section — they either panic about it or completely ignore it. Neither works.

The GK and Aptitude section is only in Stage I and carries around 20-40 marks depending on the pattern. It can fail the qualifying attempt if ignored.

What it covers:

Area What to expect
Current Affairs Health schemes, government policies, recent medical news
General English Basic grammar, comprehension, vocabulary
Reasoning & Aptitude Series, analogies, basic logical reasoning

Small practices that works for this:

  • 10-15 minutes of current affairs daily is enough. Focus specifically on health ministry updates, national health schemes, and anything related to nursing or hospitals. General world news is less likely to appear.
  • For reasoning, solve a small set of questions daily rather than studying it theory-first. It’s a skill that builds with practice, not reading.
  • English preparation mostly takes care of itself if you’re reading study material in English regularly.

Common Mistakes Aspirants Make#

1. Treating all subjects equally

Not every subject carries the same weight in the exam. Spending equal time on Biochemistry and Medical-Surgical Nursing is not a balanced strategy — it’s an inefficient one. Map your time to the actual weightage.

2. Ignoring Stage I because it’s “just qualifying”

Stage I has negative marking and a cutoff you must clear. Students who take it lightly and assume they’ll pass on nursing knowledge alone often get caught off guard by the GK and Aptitude section. Qualifying is not guaranteed — treat Stage I seriously.

3. Starting mock tests too late

Mock tests are not just for the final month. They are a diagnostic tool. Starting them early tells you where your actual gaps are, not where you think they are. Most students are surprised by what they don’t know when they sit for their first timed mock.

4. Memorizing without understanding clinical application

Stage II specifically tests clinical reasoning — what you would do in a given patient scenario, not just what a definition says. If your preparation is entirely memory-based, Stage II will feel like a different exam entirely.

5. Using too many resources

More books does not mean better preparation. It usually means scattered, incomplete preparation. Pick your resources, finish them, and revisit them. Depth over breadth.

NORCET preparation is not complicated — it just needs to start in the right direction. Know the exam, know the syllabus, pick your resources, and begin. The rest figures itself out as you go.
